If you are applying to Studienkolleg from outside the EU, the blocked account can feel like one of the most confusing parts of the process. It is not part of the Aufnahmepruefung itself, and it is usually not something the Studienkolleg checks during the academic application. It belongs to the visa and residence permit process.

That difference matters. You can prepare your documents, pass the entrance exam, and still have problems entering Germany if your proof of financing is not ready in time. For many Studienkolleg applicants, the blocked account is the most common way to prove that they can cover living costs during their first year in Germany.

This guide explains what a blocked account is, who normally needs it, who may not need it, and when you should prepare it during the Studienkolleg journey.

## What Is a Blocked Account?

A blocked account, or *Sperrkonto* in German, is a special bank account used by many international students to show proof of financial resources for Germany. You deposit a required amount before your visa appointment. After you arrive in Germany, you can usually withdraw only a limited monthly amount.

The account is "blocked" because the full amount is not freely available at once. The idea is simple: German authorities want to see that you have enough money to support yourself month by month, not only enough money for the flight and first few weeks.

The DAAD's official Study in Germany portal explains proof of financing as the document that shows you have enough money to support yourself before you begin your studies. It also lists the blocked account as one possible form of proof of financing, next to options such as parental income documents, a guarantee from someone in Germany, a bank guarantee, or a recognized scholarship notification. You can read the official overview on [Study in Germany's proof of financing page](https://www.study-in-germany.com/en/plan-your-studies/requirements/proof-of-financing/).

For Studienkolleg applicants, the blocked account is usually connected to the visa because Studienkolleg is a preparatory education path before university studies. It is not a payment to the Studienkolleg. It is your own money, held in a restricted way so that you can use it for rent, food, health insurance, transport, learning materials, and daily life after arrival.

## The Current Amount You Should Know

From 1 January 2025, the presumed annual requirement for a blocked account when applying for a visa is **EUR 11,904**. That equals **EUR 992 per month** for 12 months.

This amount can change, so do not treat an old YouTube video, WhatsApp message, or agency screenshot as your final source. Before opening the account or transferring money, check the current number on the official [Study in Germany proof of financing page](https://www.study-in-germany.com/en/plan-your-studies/requirements/proof-of-financing/) and on the website of the German embassy or consulate responsible for your visa application.

Also remember that EUR 11,904 is a visa requirement figure, not a complete personal budget. In expensive cities, your real monthly costs can be higher, especially if you pay a rental deposit, buy furniture, pay semester fees, or need private health insurance at the beginning. The blocked account proves a minimum level of financial security; it does not guarantee that every city or lifestyle will fit comfortably inside that amount.

## Do Studienkolleg Applicants Need a Blocked Account?

Many Studienkolleg applicants need a blocked account, but not everyone does. The key question is not only "Am I applying to Studienkolleg?" The better question is:

**Do I need to prove financial resources for a German visa or residence permit?**

According to Study in Germany, students are asked for proof of financing when applying for a visa and need it at the latest when applying for a residence permit in Germany. For most non-EU applicants who plan to live in Germany for Studienkolleg, that proof is required.

In practice, you will usually need a blocked account or another accepted proof of financing if:

- You are from a country whose citizens need a visa before entering Germany for study preparation.
- You are applying for a prospective student visa before receiving final admission.
- You have received admission to a Studienkolleg or foundation course and are applying for a student visa.
- You entered Germany without a visa because your citizenship allows it, but you still need to apply for a residence permit after arrival.
- Your embassy or local foreigners' office specifically asks for a blocked account, even if another form of proof might be accepted elsewhere.

The blocked account is not automatically required by every Studienkolleg, but it is commonly required by the visa process that allows you to attend Studienkolleg in Germany.

## Who Usually Does Not Need One?

You may not need a blocked account if you do not need to prove financing through that method. Common examples include:

- You are a citizen of an EU country, the European Economic Area, or Switzerland.
- You have another accepted form of financial proof, such as a recognized scholarship, a formal obligation letter from a resident in Germany, or accepted parental income documents.
- You already live in Germany with a residence status that allows you to attend Studienkolleg, and the foreigners' office does not ask for a blocked account.
- You are applying for a private Studienkolleg from abroad but will use a different proof of financing accepted by your German mission.

Study in Germany states that students from the EU, the European Economic Area, or Switzerland do not require a visa. That does not mean studying is free of living costs. It means the visa proof route is different. You still need to budget for rent, insurance, semester fees, food, books, transport, and the first weeks after arrival.

For everyone else, do not guess based on nationality alone. Visa rules depend on citizenship, purpose of stay, length of stay, and the German mission responsible for your application. The safest habit is to check the official visa page of your German embassy or consulate before you spend money on translations, providers, or appointment preparation.

## Prospective Student Visa vs Student Visa

Studienkolleg applicants often stand between two stages: not yet fully admitted, but already preparing for a German higher education path. That is why understanding the visa category matters.

Study in Germany describes two relevant visa types:

- **Prospective student visa**: for applicants who have not yet received admission to a university or foundation course.
- **Student visa**: for applicants who have received admission to a university or foundation course.

For Studienkolleg, the "foundation course" wording is important. If you have only applied and are waiting for an entrance exam invitation or admission decision, your situation may be different from someone who already has a Zulassungsbescheid or official admission to Studienkolleg. The required proof of financing can appear in both routes, but the rest of your documents may differ.

This is also why timing matters. Some students wait until they pass the Aufnahmepruefung before thinking about the blocked account. That can be too late if the embassy appointment is soon, the transfer takes time, or the provider needs additional verification. You do not need to panic and open an account too early, but you should understand the requirement months before your planned move.

## When Should You Open the Blocked Account?

The best timing depends on your visa timeline, application status, and embassy appointment availability. A practical sequence looks like this:

### 1. Check Your Visa Requirement Early

As soon as you decide to apply for Studienkolleg, check whether your citizenship requires a visa before entering Germany. Use the German mission responsible for your place of residence, not a random embassy website from another country.

At this stage, you do not necessarily need to transfer the money yet. You need to know whether proof of financing will be required and which forms are accepted.

### 2. Prepare the Money Before the Visa Appointment

If you will use a blocked account, plan the transfer well before your visa appointment. International transfers can be delayed by bank checks, missing reference numbers, weekends, public holidays, currency controls, or provider verification.

Study in Germany notes that processing can become slower around the beginning of the semester. That is exactly when many Studienkolleg applicants are trying to arrange visas, housing, health insurance, and travel at the same time.

### 3. Keep the Confirmation Document

After the account is opened and funded, you should receive a confirmation document. This is usually what you include in your visa file as proof that the required amount has been deposited.

Save it carefully and bring it to the visa appointment in the format requested by the embassy. Some missions want printed documents, some use online uploads, and some ask for both.

### 4. Activate Access After Arrival

Once you arrive in Germany, you will usually need to activate withdrawals, connect the blocked account to a current account, complete identity checks, or submit your German address. Do this early because rent, insurance, and food costs begin immediately.

The blocked account is only one part of arrival administration. You may also need city registration, health insurance proof, a residence permit appointment, enrollment documents, and a normal German bank account.

## What Counts as Proof of Financing Besides a Blocked Account?

A blocked account is common, but it is not the only possible proof. Depending on the embassy or foreigners' office, other accepted options may include:

- Documents showing your parents' income and financial assets.
- A formal declaration of commitment from someone living in Germany.
- A recognized scholarship notification.
- A bank guarantee.
- Other documents specifically accepted by your German mission.

The important word is **accepted**. A document that looks strong to you may not be accepted by the visa officer. For example, a normal bank statement from your home country might not be enough if the mission asks specifically for a blocked account. A sponsor letter from a relative may not be enough if it is not in the official format.

Before choosing a route, compare three things: the German mission's visa checklist, the Study in Germany proof of financing guidance, and any instructions from your university or Studienkolleg. If they differ, follow the binding instructions from the German mission or foreigners' office.

## Common Mistakes Studienkolleg Applicants Make

### Waiting Until the Admission Letter Arrives

Some students only start financial planning after they receive Studienkolleg admission. The problem is that visa processing can take months, and the blocked account is only one item in the file. You should already know your financing plan while applying.

### Confusing Application Fees With Living Cost Proof

Uni-assist fees, semester fees, private Studienkolleg tuition, and entrance exam costs are separate from the blocked account. Paying one of them does not replace proof that you can cover living expenses in Germany.

### Assuming the Studienkolleg Will Decide the Visa Rule

The Studienkolleg decides academic admission. The embassy, consulate, and foreigners' office decide visa and residence requirements. A Studienkolleg admission letter can support your visa application, but it does not remove the need to prove financing.

### Forgetting Extra Start-Up Costs

Your first month in Germany is often more expensive than a normal month. You may need a rental deposit, first rent, temporary accommodation, public transport, household items, SIM card, insurance payments, and document fees. Keep extra money outside the blocked account if possible.

### Using Outdated Amounts

The blocked-account amount has changed over time. If you prepare with an old number, your visa file may be incomplete. Always check the current amount shortly before transferring money.

## How This Fits Into Your Studienkolleg Timeline

Here is a realistic planning order for many non-EU applicants:

1. Check whether your school certificate leads to direct university admission or Studienkolleg.
2. Choose the right Schwerpunktkurs and target Studienkollegs.
3. Check the visa category and proof-of-financing requirement for your citizenship.
4. Prepare your academic documents, translations, APS certificate if required, and German certificate.
5. Apply to Studienkolleg or through uni-assist.
6. Prepare for the Aufnahmepruefung.
7. Arrange blocked account or another accepted financial proof before the visa file is due.
8. Attend the visa appointment with complete documents.
9. Travel to Germany, register your address, activate access to money, and handle residence permit steps.

This order may change depending on your country and admission route, but the main point is stable: financial proof is not something to leave for the final week.

## Final Advice

If you are a non-EU Studienkolleg applicant, assume that proof of financing will be part of your path until an official source tells you otherwise. For many students, that means opening and funding a blocked account with the current required annual amount.

Use official sources for the number and the accepted document format. The [Study in Germany visa page](https://www.study-in-germany.com/en/plan-your-studies/requirements/visa/) is useful for understanding visa categories, and the [proof of financing page](https://www.study-in-germany.com/en/plan-your-studies/requirements/proof-of-financing/) explains the blocked account and current annual requirement. Your German embassy or consulate is the binding source for your exact visa checklist.

The blocked account is not the hardest intellectual part of Studienkolleg. The German exam, mathematics preparation, and the Aufnahmepruefung will demand more study time. But the blocked account is a deadline-sensitive administrative step. Handle it early, verify the amount, keep your confirmation document, and make sure your financial proof is ready before your visa process depends on it.
